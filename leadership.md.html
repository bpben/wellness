Leadership is often recognized as one of the most important drivers of organizational change.[1] Much of the guidance literature emphasizes leadership as one of the major components for building effective wellness programs.[2][3] Having management involved in health promotion efforts has been linked with increased impact in terms of self-reported health and absenteeism.[4] Berry et. al. has the engagement of multiple levels of leadership as its first pillar of an effectiveness wellness program.[5] Research has suggested the idea of the wellness “champion”, or a local leader that promotes engagement with the wellness program among coworkers.[6] By engaging middle-management or supervisors as champions or training them in the importance of health promotion, programs may achieve better reach among employees. Some key components of leadership engagement are contained in the Leading By Example scale.[7]
